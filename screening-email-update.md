# Screening Email Update — Deal Briefing for Partners

> **PURPOSE**: Produce a concise email that gives Partners full context on a screening-stage deal in under 2 minutes of reading. The recipients have NO prior context — the email must stand alone. This is not a summary of the memo; it is a decision-oriented briefing that tells the reader what the deal is, why it matters, what concerns exist, and what we plan to find out next.

## When to Use

- After completing a target screening memo (using `target-screening-lite.md` or equivalent)
- Before a first management meeting or site visit
- When Partners need to be brought up to speed on a new deal quickly
- When preparing a pre-IC circulation email

## Inputs Required

1. **Completed screening memo** — the source of all facts and figures
2. **Recipients** — who is this going to (Partners, IC members, deal team)
3. **Meeting context** — what is happening next (e.g., "meeting with founder next week")
4. **Cross-deal angles** — any portfolio synergies worth flagging (optional)

## Email Structure

Write in natural paragraphs, not bullet dumps. Each section header below is a **bolded inline label** within the email body — not a formal heading. The email should read like a well-structured note from an Associate to a time-poor Partner.

### Subject Line

Format: `[Company Name] — [Purpose] ([Meeting/Timeline Context])`

Example: `Expo AV — Pre-Meeting Briefing (Meeting w/ Founder Next Week)`

### Opening Line

One sentence. State what you're sharing and why.

Example: *"Sharing a quick briefing on [Company] ahead of our meeting with [Person/Role] next week."*

### Section 1: What It Is

**Bold label: "What it is:"**

3-4 sentences max. Cover: what the company does, how long it's been operating, revenue scale, headcount, key positioning (venue partnerships, market position, regulatory moat — whatever is relevant), profitability at EBITDA level, and any structural characteristic that shapes the thesis (e.g., project-based vs recurring, founder-led, asset-light vs heavy).

The reader should be able to picture the business after this paragraph.

### Section 2: The Opportunity

**Bold label: "The opportunity:"**

2-3 sentences. Cover: what we are buying (control, minority, secondary), from whom, at what price, why the seller is selling, and any concurrent capital raise or transaction structure.

### Section 3: The Thesis / Strong Points

**Bold label: "Why this is interesting:"**

3-5 sentences. This is the positive case. Cover: why this market is attractive, what the company's moat or defensibility is, what makes the entry terms compelling, and what the exit story looks like. Be specific — name the moat mechanism, the entry multiple, the re-rating catalyst. This is where you make the reader lean in.

### Section 4: Growth Angles

Include **one paragraph per distinct growth angle**, each with a bolded inline label. Common growth angles include:

- **IP / product angle:** If the company is building owned IP, products, or platforms beyond its core service. Describe what exists (operating vs pre-revenue), ownership structure, revenue targets with timelines, and key risks.
- **Overseas / regional growth angle:** If the company is expanding geographically. For each market: where, why that market, what they plan to do there, expected revenue, by when, and how credible the plan is. Name the partners or anchors if they exist.
- **Buy-and-build angle:** If there are bolt-on targets or consolidation plays. Name the targets or target profile.
- **Operational improvement angle:** If there are margin or efficiency levers a new owner can pull.
- **Cross-deal / portfolio angle:** If there is synergy with an existing portfolio company or active deal. Be specific about what the synergy is and how it would work.

Each growth paragraph should follow this structure: *What they are doing → Why that market/opportunity → Expected revenue and timeline → How credible it is (partner relationships, contracts, or lack thereof).*

### Section 5: Core Concerns

**Bold label: "Core concerns at this stage:"**

Bulleted list, 4-6 items. Each bullet: **bold the risk label**, then 1-2 sentences explaining why it matters. These should be the issues that would change the thesis if they go the wrong way — not generic risk language. Prioritise by severity.

Include concerns across these dimensions:
- **Revenue concentration / customer risk**
- **Revenue trajectory** (growing, declining, structural vs cyclical)
- **Entity structure / value leakage** (if relevant — e.g., IP in separate entities)
- **Working capital / cash conversion**
- **Information gaps** (e.g., key documents not yet obtained)
- **Governance / control** (minority position, no board seat, etc.)

### Section 6: What We Want to Clarify

**Bold label: "What we want to clarify with [Person] next week:"**

Numbered list, 5-8 items. These are the specific questions for the management meeting. Each should be a concrete, answerable question — not a vague topic. Frame them as what you'll ask, not what you need to know.

Good: *"Why did revenue decline FY24→FY25 — structural or one-off?"*
Bad: *"Revenue trends"*

Prioritise by: (1) thesis-killing questions first, (2) valuation-impacting questions, (3) operational detail.

### Closing

One line: reference the attached screening memo for detail, offer to walk through before the meeting.

## Tone & Style Rules

1. **No prior context.** Write as if the reader has never heard of this company. Spell out the business, the sector, the deal structure.
2. **Concise but complete.** The full email should be readable in under 2 minutes. Every sentence must earn its place.
3. **Specific over generic.** Name the revenue figure, the client, the venue, the IP event. Don't say "key client relationships" — say "Constellar is 42% of revenue."
4. **Opinionated where warranted.** If a growth plan is not credible, say so. If the entry price is fair but not cheap, say so. Don't hedge with "potentially" or "could possibly."
5. **Acronyms are fine.** Use EBITDA, DSO, MOIC, IRR, MICE, AVL without spelling out. The audience is PE professionals.
6. **No formatting overkill.** Bold labels for sections, bullets for concerns and clarification questions. No headers, no tables, no horizontal rules. This is an email, not a report.

```
Subject: [Company] — Pre-Meeting Briefing (Meeting w/ [Person] [Date])

Hi [Recipients],

Sharing a quick briefing on [Company] ahead of our meeting with [Person/Role] next week.

**What it is:** [3-4 sentences on the business]

**The opportunity:** [2-3 sentences on deal structure and entry]

**Why this is interesting:** [3-5 sentences on thesis and strong points]

**[Growth angle 1 label]:** [Paragraph on first growth dimension]

**[Growth angle 2 label]:** [Paragraph on second growth dimension]

**[Cross-deal angle label]:** [1-2 sentences if applicable]

- **[Risk 1].** [1-2 sentences]
- **[Risk 2].** [1-2 sentences]
- **[Risk 3].** [1-2 sentences]
```

## Quality Checklist

Before sending, verify:
- [ ] A reader with zero context can understand the deal after reading this email
- [ ] Revenue figures, entry price, and EBITDA are all stated with sources
- [ ] Every growth angle includes expected revenue AND timeline
- [ ] Concerns are specific and falsifiable, not generic
- [ ] Clarification questions are concrete and answerable in a single meeting
- [ ] The email is under 600 words (excluding subject line and sign-off)
- [ ] No fully-spelt acronyms (use EBITDA, not "Earnings Before Interest, Tax, Depreciation and Amortisation")
- [ ] Cross-deal angles are mentioned if they exist
